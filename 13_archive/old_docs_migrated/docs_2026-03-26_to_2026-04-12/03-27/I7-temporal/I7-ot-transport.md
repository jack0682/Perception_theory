## 1. Diagnosis: Why Persist Has Zero Results

The Canonical Spec v2.0 (Section 7.1, Status Note) acknowledges: *"The persistence predicate has zero proved results across all iterations. This is the theory's largest gap."*

Three structural reasons:

### 1.1. External Dependence Breaks Self-Referentiality

The provisional transport kernel (Spec §9.5) depends on:
- $\Psi_{t \to s}$: external predicted spatial correspondence (e.g., optical flow)
- $\varphi_t(x)$: external feature representations

These are not generated by the cohesion field. The within-time operators (Cl, D, C) are all self-referential — they depend on $u_t$ and $\mathbf{N}_t$ only. Transport is the sole operator that breaks this pattern. Without self-referentiality, there is no structural relationship between the transport kernel and the energy landscape, so no variational tools can be brought to bear on Persist.

### 1.2. No Variational Characterization of Transport

The transport kernel $\mathbf{M}_{t \to s}$ appears in the energy ($\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{tr}}$) as a *given* kernel over which the field is optimized, not as something that is itself variationally characterized. The energy minimization fixes $u$ given $\mathbf{M}$, but $\mathbf{M}$ is not simultaneously optimized. This means there is no variational equation that $\mathbf{M}$ satisfies — and hence no PDE/variational tools for proving properties of $\mathbf{M}$ at formation-structured fields.

### 1.3. No Metric Structure on the Space of Formations

Persist asks whether the core is "structurally inherited." But there is no metric that quantifies structural distance between $u_t$ and $u_s$ in a way that respects the relational organization. Without such a metric, "gentle" vs. "violent" transport is undefined, and one cannot state quantitative persistence conditions.

**The OT reframing addresses all three problems simultaneously.**

### Critical Structural Matches

**Match 1 (Sub-stochasticity ↔ Partial OT).** SCC's E1 axiom ($\sum_y M(x,y) \leq 1$) is precisely the condition that the transport plan is *partial*: some cohesive mass may dissipate without being received. In partial OT, this is modeled by allowing the transport plan's marginals to be *dominated by* (not equal to) the source and target measures. The match is not an analogy — it is a mathematical identity.

**Match 2 (Entropic regularization ↔ Soft transport).** SCC's E2 axiom (non-injectivity) and the provisional kernel's softmax form (§9.5) are structurally identical to entropic OT. In entropic OT with regularization parameter $\varepsilon > 0$, the optimal plan has the form $\pi^*(x,y) = a(x) \cdot b(y) \cdot e^{-c(x,y)/\varepsilon}$, which is smooth, positive, and non-injective — exactly the structural properties SCC requires.

**Match 3 (Self-referential cost ↔ Novel OT).** This is where SCC *departs* from standard OT. In classical OT, the cost $c(x,y)$ is exogenous — it does not depend on the transport plan or the measures. In SCC, the cost must depend on the cohesion fields $u_t, u_s$ (to achieve self-referentiality). This makes the OT problem a *fixed-point* problem: the optimal $\mathbf{M}$ depends on the cost, which depends on the fields, which are optimized given $\mathbf{M}$. This is mathematically novel.

## 3. Reformulation: $\mathbf{M}_{t \to s}$ as Entropic Partial OT

### 3.1. The Cohesion Measures

At each time $t$, define the **cohesion measure** on $X_t$:

$$
\mu_t = \sum_{x \in X_t} u_t(x)\, \delta_x
$$

The total mass is $|\mu_t| = \sum_x u_t(x) = m$ (by the volume constraint). The cohesion measure is the natural "mass distribution" of the formation: sites with high cohesion carry more mass; exterior sites carry negligible mass.

### 3.2. The Self-Referential Transport Cost

The cost of transporting cohesive content from site $x$ at time $t$ to site $y$ at time $s$ is:

$$
c(x,y;\, u_t, u_s) = d_{X}(x,y)^2 + \lambda_{\mathrm{struct}} \cdot d_{\mathrm{struct}}(x, y;\, u_t, u_s)
$$

where:

- $d_X(x,y)^2$ is the spatial cost (squared distance in the ambient space, or graph distance on $X_t \cup X_s$)
- $d_{\mathrm{struct}}(x, y;\, u_t, u_s)$ is the **structural dissimilarity cost**, measuring how differently $x$ and $y$ are positioned within their respective formations

The structural cost is what makes this self-referential. Candidate forms (all depending only on $u_t, u_s, \mathbf{N}$):

**Option A (Local profile distance):**
$$
d_{\mathrm{struct}}(x,y) = \left\| \frac{(P_t u_t)(\cdot\, ; x)}{(P_t u_t)(x)} - \frac{(P_s u_s)(\cdot\, ; y)}{(P_s u_s)(y)} \right\|^2
$$
where $(P_t u_t)(\cdot\, ; x)$ is the normalized local cohesion profile around $x$. This compares how $x$ sits within its local relational neighborhood at time $t$ to how $y$ sits within its neighborhood at time $s$.

**Option B (Morphological role distance):**
$$
d_{\mathrm{struct}}(x,y) = \big| u_t(x) - u_s(y) \big|^2 + \big| g_t(x) - g_s(y) \big|^2
$$
where $g_t(x)$ is the gradient indicator (§5.3). This says: transport prefers to match core-to-core, boundary-to-boundary, exterior-to-exterior. A core site at time $t$ should be received by a core site at time $s$, not by a boundary site.

**Option C (Distinction-weighted):**
$$
d_{\mathrm{struct}}(x,y) = \big| \mathbf{D}_t(x; 1-u_t) - \mathbf{D}_s(y; 1-u_s) \big|^2
$$
This uses the distinction operator to match sites by their structural asymmetry with respect to the exterior.

**Recommendation.** Option B is simplest and most directly interpretable. It depends only on $u_t, u_s$, and $\mathbf{N}_t, \mathbf{N}_s$ (through $g_t, g_s$). It preserves morphological role under transport. Options A and C are richer but introduce computational complexity; they should be treated as refinements for later investigation. All three options achieve self-referentiality.

### 3.3. The Entropic Partial OT Problem

The transport kernel is defined as the solution to:

$$
\mathbf{M}_{t \to s}^* = \arg\min_{\pi \geq 0} \left\{ \sum_{x,y} \pi(x,y)\, c(x,y;\, u_t, u_s) + \varepsilon\, \mathrm{KL}(\pi \| \mu_t \otimes \mu_s) + \lambda_{\mathrm{partial}} \cdot \mathrm{Pen}(\pi) \right\}
$$

subject to:
- $\sum_y \pi(x,y) \leq u_t(x)$ for all $x$ (sub-stochastic source marginal → E1)
- $\sum_x \pi(x,y) \leq u_s(y)$ for all $y$ (sub-stochastic target marginal)

where:

- $\varepsilon > 0$ is the entropic regularization parameter (controls softness of transport → E2)
- $\mathrm{KL}(\pi \| \mu_t \otimes \mu_s)$ is the Kullback-Leibler divergence from the product measure (entropic regularizer)
- $\mathrm{Pen}(\pi)$ penalizes mass destruction/creation:
$$
\mathrm{Pen}(\pi) = \sum_x \left( u_t(x) - \sum_y \pi(x,y) \right) + \sum_y \left( u_s(y) - \sum_x \pi(x,y) \right)
$$
This is the total unmatched mass at source and target.

### 3.4. Why This Satisfies E1–E4

| Axiom | How Satisfied |
|-------|---------------|
| **E1** (Sub-stochasticity) | By construction: marginal constraint $\sum_y \pi(x,y) \leq u_t(x) \leq 1$ |
| **E2** (Non-injectivity) | Entropic regularization $\varepsilon > 0$ produces strictly positive, smooth plans — inherently non-injective |
| **E3** (Core inheritance) | Not an axiom of the kernel (reclassified to solution constraint in v2.0). But the structural cost $d_{\mathrm{struct}}$ *incentivizes* core-to-core matching: core sites have similar $(u, g)$ profiles, so their mutual cost is low |
| **E4** (Structural sensitivity) | The structural cost $d_{\mathrm{struct}}$ makes $\pi^*$ depend on the cohesion field's structure, not just spatial proximity |

### 3.5. Self-Referentiality as Fixed Point

The full self-referential transport problem is:

$$
\text{Find } (u_t^*, u_s^*, \mathbf{M}^*) \text{ such that:}
$$

1. $u_t^*$ minimizes $\mathcal{E}(\cdot\, ; \mathbf{M}^*)$ at time $t$ on $\Sigma_m$
2. $u_s^*$ minimizes $\mathcal{E}(\cdot\, ; \mathbf{M}^*)$ at time $s$ on $\Sigma_m$
3. $\mathbf{M}^* = \mathbf{M}_{t \to s}^*[u_t^*, u_s^*]$ solves the entropic partial OT with cost $c(\cdot, \cdot\, ; u_t^*, u_s^*)$

This is a **coupled fixed-point problem**: the fields determine the cost, the cost determines the optimal transport, and the transport enters the energy that determines the fields.

**This is mathematically novel in OT.** Standard OT treats the cost as exogenous. Self-referential OT — where the cost depends on the measures being transported — has no established theory. SCC would be the first substantive application.

## 4. The Cohesion-Weighted Wasserstein Distance

### 4.1. Definition

Given two cohesion measures $\mu_t, \mu_s$ (with possibly different total masses), define the **cohesion-weighted Wasserstein distance**:

$$
\mathcal{W}_c(\mu_t, \mu_s) = \inf_{\pi \in \Pi_{\leq}(\mu_t, \mu_s)} \left\{ \int c(x,y;\, u_t, u_s)\, d\pi(x,y) + \lambda_{\mathrm{partial}} \cdot \left( |\mu_t| + |\mu_s| - 2|\pi| \right) \right\}
$$

where $\Pi_{\leq}(\mu_t, \mu_s)$ is the set of sub-couplings (measures $\pi$ on $X_t \times X_s$ with $\pi_1 \leq \mu_t$, $\pi_2 \leq \mu_s$), and $|\pi| = \sum_{x,y} \pi(x,y)$ is the total transported mass.

This is a **partial Wasserstein distance** with self-referential cost. When $\mu_t$ and $\mu_s$ have equal total mass and the inequality constraints are tight, it reduces to the standard Wasserstein-2 distance (with modified cost).

### 4.2. Properties

Assuming the cost $c$ is a metric (or at least satisfies the triangle inequality after taking square root), $\mathcal{W}_c$ satisfies:

1. **Non-negativity:** $\mathcal{W}_c(\mu_t, \mu_s) \geq 0$, with equality iff $\mu_t = \mu_s$ (as measures on a common space)
2. **Sensitivity to morphological role:** Two formations with the same spatial support but different core/boundary structure have $\mathcal{W}_c > 0$ (because $d_{\mathrm{struct}} > 0$)
3. **Sensitivity to mass change:** If cohesion is created or destroyed between $t$ and $s$, the penalty term $\lambda_{\mathrm{partial}}(|\mu_t| + |\mu_s| - 2|\pi|)$ contributes to $\mathcal{W}_c$

### 4.3. What $\mathcal{W}_c$ Gives the Theory

$\mathcal{W}_c$ is the **missing metric on the space of formations.** It quantifies:
- How much a formation has moved (spatial cost)
- How much its internal structure has changed (structural cost)
- How much cohesive content has been gained or lost (partial transport penalty)

This is precisely what Persist needs: a quantitative notion of "how much has changed between times."

## 5. The "Gentle Transport" Condition

### 5.1. Definition

A transport from $\mu_t$ to $\mu_s$ is **gentle** if:

$$
\mathcal{W}_c(\mu_t, \mu_s) < \Delta E_{\min}
$$

where $\Delta E_{\min}$ is the **minimal energy barrier height** of the formation at time $t$ — the smallest energy increase required to escape the metastable basin containing $u_t^*$.

**Interpretation:** Gentle transport means the formation has changed less (in the Wasserstein sense) than the amount needed to leave its energy basin. The formation's "identity" — its basin of attraction in the energy landscape — has been preserved, even if its shape, position, or boundary have shifted.

### 5.2. Energy Barrier Characterization

The energy barrier at a metastable minimizer $u^*$ is:

$$
\Delta E_{\min}(u^*) = \min_{\gamma : u^* \to \partial B(u^*)} \max_{\tau \in [0,1]} \mathcal{E}(\gamma(\tau)) - \mathcal{E}(u^*)
$$

where $B(u^*)$ is the basin of attraction of $u^*$ under gradient flow, and $\gamma$ ranges over continuous paths from $u^*$ to the basin boundary.

From T7-Enhanced (Spec §13): SCC formations have *strictly larger* energy barriers than corresponding Allen-Cahn formations, due to the positive-definite closure Hessian contribution. This means gentle transport is *easier* to achieve in SCC than in Allen-Cahn — the self-referential closure actively protects formation identity.

### 5.3. Quantitative Bound (Conjectural)

**Conjecture (Gentle Transport Bound).** For the sigmoid closure realization with $a_{\mathrm{cl}} < 4$ and the morphological role cost (Option B), the energy barrier satisfies:

$$
\Delta E_{\min}(u^*) \geq \lambda_{\mathrm{cl}} \cdot \frac{(4 - a_{\mathrm{cl}})^2}{16} \cdot \|\hat{u} - u^*_{\mathrm{saddle}}\|^2 + \Gamma\text{-convergence corrections}
$$

where the first term comes from the closure Hessian contribution $2(I - J_{\mathrm{Cl}})^T(I - J_{\mathrm{Cl}})$ with eigenvalues bounded below by $(1 - a_{\mathrm{cl}}/4)^2$. The $\Gamma$-convergence corrections come from the interface energy in the sharp-interface limit (T11).

If this bound holds, then gentle transport is achievable whenever:
$$
\mathcal{W}_c(\mu_t, \mu_s) < \lambda_{\mathrm{cl}} \cdot \frac{(4 - a_{\mathrm{cl}})^2}{16} \cdot \|\hat{u} - u^*_{\mathrm{saddle}}\|^2
$$

This gives a **computable criterion** for persistence: measure the Wasserstein distance between consecutive cohesion fields and compare to the energy barrier.

## 6. Pathway to the First Persist Theorem

### 6.1. Theorem Statement (Target)

**Theorem (Gentle Transport → Core Inheritance).** Let $u_t^*$ be a metastable minimizer of $\mathcal{E}_t$ on $\Sigma_m$ with energy barrier $\Delta E_{\min}(u_t^*) > 0$. Let $u_s$ be a cohesion field at time $s$ with $\mathcal{W}_c(\mu_t^*, \mu_s) < \Delta E_{\min}(u_t^*)$. Let $u_s^*$ be the result of running the projected gradient flow on $\mathcal{E}_s$ initialized at the transported field $\mathbf{M}^*_{t \to s} \# u_t^*$. Then:

$$
\mathrm{Core}_s(u_s^*) \supseteq \mathbf{M}^*_{t \to s}[\mathrm{Core}_t(u_t^*)]_{\theta'}
$$

for a threshold $\theta' < \theta_{\mathrm{core}}$ depending on $\mathcal{W}_c / \Delta E_{\min}$. In particular, $\mathsf{Persist}_{\{t,s\}} > 0$.

**In words:** If the formation hasn't changed too much (gentle transport), and we re-optimize the energy at time $s$ starting from the transported field, then the core of $u_t^*$ is inherited — it maps to the core of $u_s^*$ (possibly at a slightly relaxed threshold).

### 6.2. Proof Strategy (Three Steps)

**Step 1: Transported field is in the basin.**

*Claim:* If $\mathcal{W}_c(\mu_t^*, \mu_s) < \Delta E_{\min}(u_t^*)$ and the energy landscape at time $s$ is sufficiently close to time $t$ (controlled by the time step), then the transported field $\tilde{u}_s = \mathbf{M}^*_{t \to s} \# u_t^*$ lies in the basin of attraction of a metastable minimizer $u_s^*$ of $\mathcal{E}_s$.

*Proof ingredients:*
- The gentle transport condition bounds $\|\tilde{u}_s - u_t^*\|$ (via the relationship between Wasserstein distance and $L^2$ distance on discrete spaces)
- The energy barrier being larger than the Wasserstein distance means the transported field has not crossed the energy saddle
- This requires the energy landscapes at times $t$ and $s$ to be comparable — a condition on how fast the relational structure $\mathbf{N}_t$ changes

*Importable OT theorem:* **Stability of optimal transport plans under perturbation of measures** (Villani, Theorem 5.20). If $\mu_t^{(n)} \to \mu_t$ weakly, then $\pi_n \to \pi$ (any cluster point of optimal plans is an optimal plan for the limit measures). This gives continuity of the transported field in the source/target measures.

**Step 2: Gradient flow converges to a nearby minimizer.**

*Claim:* The projected gradient flow on $\mathcal{E}_s$ initialized at $\tilde{u}_s$ converges to $u_s^*$, the metastable minimizer in whose basin $\tilde{u}_s$ lies.

*Proof ingredients:*
- T14 (Spec §13): Gradient flow converges to a critical point (Łojasiewicz)
- Since $\tilde{u}_s$ is in the basin of $u_s^*$, the gradient flow converges specifically to $u_s^*$
- The convergence rate is geometric (from the contraction properties, A3)

This step uses existing proved results. No new mathematics needed.

**Step 3: Core inheritance under bounded perturbation.**

*Claim:* If $\|u_s^* - \tilde{u}_s\|$ is small (bounded by the gradient flow convergence) and $\tilde{u}_s$ was produced by optimal transport from $u_t^*$, then $\mathrm{Core}_s(u_s^*)$ contains the image of $\mathrm{Core}_t(u_t^*)$ under transport (at a relaxed threshold).

*Proof ingredients:*
- The structural cost (Option B) ensures that core sites at time $t$ are matched to high-cohesion sites at time $s$ (since $|u_t(x) - u_s(y)|^2$ is minimized when core maps to core)
- The gradient flow preserves core structure: sites that start with high cohesion ($\tilde{u}_s(y) \geq \theta_{\mathrm{core}} - \delta$) remain at high cohesion after convergence to $u_s^*$ (because the energy basin favors the formation structure)
- Quantitatively: $u_s^*(y) \geq \tilde{u}_s(y) - \|u_s^* - \tilde{u}_s\|_\infty \geq (\theta_{\mathrm{core}} - \delta) - \text{flow correction}$

*Importable OT theorem:* **Monotonicity of optimal transport maps** (Brenier's theorem generalization). In 1D or with convex cost, optimal transport preserves ordering. For the structural cost, this gives: if $u_t(x_1) > u_t(x_2)$, then the transport plan concentrates mass from $x_1$ on sites $y$ with $u_s(y) > u_s(\text{image of } x_2)$. This is core-to-core preference as a *theorem*, not an assumption.

### 6.3. What Remains Open

| Component | Status | Effort |
|-----------|--------|--------|

## 7. Importable OT Theorems

These are established OT results that transfer to the SCC setting with stated modifications.

### 7.1. Existence of Optimal Plans (Kantorovich)

**Classical result:** For lower semicontinuous cost $c$ on a compact product space, the infimum in the Kantorovich problem is attained — an optimal transport plan exists.

**SCC import:** For fixed $(u_t, u_s)$, the entropic partial OT problem (§3.3) has a unique solution $\pi^*_\varepsilon$ (uniqueness from strict convexity of KL divergence). The plan is strictly positive and has the Sinkhorn form:

$$
\pi^*_\varepsilon(x,y) = a(x) \cdot b(y) \cdot e^{-c(x,y)/\varepsilon}
$$

where $(a, b)$ are the Sinkhorn scaling factors.

**Caveat:** This is for *fixed* cost. The self-referential case (cost depends on the plan's marginals) requires a fixed-point argument on top of this.

### 7.2. Sinkhorn Algorithm Convergence

**Classical result:** The Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm converges linearly to the optimal entropic transport plan, with rate depending on $\varepsilon$ and the cost matrix.

**SCC import:** For each inner loop (fixed cost), transport can be computed efficiently via Sinkhorn iterations. Complexity: $O(n^2 / \varepsilon)$ per iteration, where $n = |X_t|$.

**Significance for SCC:** The provisional kernel (§9.5) requires computing a softmax — essentially one Sinkhorn iteration. The OT reformulation generalizes this to the full Sinkhorn fixed point, giving a principled (not ad hoc) normalization.

### 7.3. Stability of Optimal Transport (Villani)

**Classical result:** If $\mu_n \to \mu$ and $\nu_n \to \nu$ weakly, and $c$ is continuous, then any accumulation point of optimal plans $\pi_n$ is an optimal plan for $(\mu, \nu)$.

**SCC import:** Small perturbations of the cohesion fields produce small perturbations of the optimal transport plan. This is the key *continuity* result needed for Step 1 of the Persist theorem.

**Caveat:** The self-referential cost means "small perturbation of fields" also changes the cost. Need: continuity of $c(x,y; u_t, u_s)$ in $(u_t, u_s)$ — which holds for all three proposed cost options (A, B, C), since they depend continuously on $u$ and $\mathbf{N}$.

### 7.4. Displacement Interpolation (McCann)

**Classical result:** For the Wasserstein-2 distance on $\mathbb{R}^d$, the geodesic between measures $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$ is given by $\mu_\tau = (T_\tau)_\# \mu_0$, where $T_\tau = (1-\tau) \mathrm{id} + \tau T$ and $T$ is the optimal map.

**SCC import (partial):** On discrete spaces, there is no unique optimal map (only plans). But the Wasserstein geodesic still exists as a measure-valued curve. This gives a canonical "interpolation" between formations at successive times — a candidate for the dynamic update law (Spec §11.2, item 4).

**Caveat:** Does not directly transfer to self-referential cost. But it provides the geometric intuition: the "natural" between-time evolution follows the Wasserstein geodesic.

### 7.5. Brenier's Theorem (Monotone Transport)

**Classical result:** For absolutely continuous measures on $\mathbb{R}^d$ with quadratic cost, the optimal transport map is the gradient of a convex function.

**SCC import (limited):** On discrete spaces, Brenier's theorem does not directly apply. However, the structural cost (Option B) creates a preference for "monotone" transport: core maps to core, boundary to boundary. In 1D discrete settings, this can be made rigorous via the monotone rearrangement.

**Significance:** This is the mathematical backbone of E3 (core inheritance) — not as an axiom, but as a *consequence* of the optimal transport with structural cost.

### 7.6. Partial OT (Figalli, Caffarelli)

**Classical result:** In partial optimal transport, one transports a prescribed fraction of the total mass. The optimal partial plan transports the "easiest" mass first (lowest-cost regions). Existence, uniqueness (for strictly convex costs), and regularity results are established.

**SCC import:** The sub-stochastic constraint (E1) fits directly into the partial OT framework. The penalty $\lambda_{\mathrm{partial}}$ controls how much mass is allowed to dissipate. At $\lambda_{\mathrm{partial}} \to \infty$, we recover full transport (all mass must be matched); at $\lambda_{\mathrm{partial}} = 0$, no transport is required.

**Key insight for SCC:** Partial OT naturally transports the *core first* (because core-to-core matching has the lowest cost, due to the structural cost term). Boundary and exterior sites are the first to be "left behind." This is E3 as a theorem, not an assumption.

### 7.7. Wasserstein Gradient Flows (Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto)

**Classical result (JKO scheme):** The gradient flow of an energy functional $F$ with respect to the Wasserstein-2 metric is:

$$
\mu^{(k+1)} = \arg\min_\mu \left\{ F(\mu) + \frac{1}{2\tau} W_2^2(\mu, \mu^{(k)}) \right\}
$$

This characterizes many PDEs (heat equation, porous medium equation, Fokker-Planck) as Wasserstein gradient flows.

**SCC import (major):** The SCC energy $\mathcal{E}$ can be viewed as the functional $F$, and the between-time evolution can be characterized as a JKO step:

$$
u_{s}^* = \arg\min_{u \in \Sigma_m} \left\{ \mathcal{E}_s(u) + \frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{tr}}}{2\tau} \mathcal{W}_c^2(\mu_t^*, \mu_u) \right\}
$$

where $\mu_u = u \cdot \mathrm{vol}_{X_s}$. This *unifies* the transport energy term ($\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{tr}}$) with the Wasserstein distance: the transport term in the SCC energy *is* the JKO proximal term.

**This is the deepest structural match.** It says: SCC's temporal theory is a Wasserstein gradient flow of the within-time energy. The transport kernel is not an additional object — it is the *shadow* of the Wasserstein proximal step.

## 8. The JKO Unification: Transport Energy as Wasserstein Proximal

### 8.1. The Key Identification

Compare the SCC transport energy (Spec §8.5):

$$
\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{tr}}(u_t, u_s) = \sum_{x,y} \mathbf{M}(x,y)\, \omega(u_t(x), u_s(y))\, (u_s(y) - u_t(x))^2
$$

with the JKO proximal term:

$$
\frac{1}{2\tau} \mathcal{W}_c^2(\mu_t, \mu_s) = \frac{1}{2\tau} \inf_\pi \sum_{x,y} \pi(x,y)\, c(x,y)
$$

If we set $\omega(a,b) = 1$, $c(x,y) = (u_s(y) - u_t(x))^2$ (purely structural cost), and $\pi = \mathbf{M}$, then **$\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{tr}}$ is the Wasserstein cost evaluated at a specific (not necessarily optimal) plan.**

The OT reformulation says: instead of using a fixed $\mathbf{M}$ and computing the transport energy, *optimize over $\mathbf{M}$* to find the minimal-cost transport. The transport energy becomes:

$$
\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\mathrm{OT}}(u_t, u_s) = \inf_{\pi \in \Pi_{\leq}(\mu_t, \mu_s)} \sum_{x,y} \pi(x,y)\, c(x,y;\, u_t, u_s) + \varepsilon\, \mathrm{KL}(\pi \| \mu_t \otimes \mu_s) + \lambda_{\mathrm{partial}} \cdot \mathrm{Pen}(\pi)
$$

This is a *lower bound* on the current $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{tr}}$ (since optimizing over $\pi$ can only decrease the cost). The optimal $\pi^*$ is precisely $\mathbf{M}^*_{t \to s}$.

### 8.2. Consequences

1. **$\mathbf{M}$ is no longer a separate input.** It is *derived* from the variational principle. The theory's formal universe can be simplified: the transport kernel is the solution to the inner OT problem, not an independent primitive.

2. **The transport term becomes differentiable in $u$.** By the envelope theorem, $\nabla_u \mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\mathrm{OT}}$ exists and can be computed via the Sinkhorn dual variables. This enables gradient-based optimization of the full energy including transport.

3. **Temporal evolution is a Wasserstein gradient flow.** The sequence $u_0^*, u_1^*, u_2^*, \ldots$ of formation fields can be characterized as the discrete-time Wasserstein gradient flow of the within-time energy, connecting SCC to the JKO framework.

### 8.3. Impact on the Formal Universe

**Proposed modification (for discussion, not unilateral):**

The formal universe currently includes $\{\mathbf{M}_{t \to s}\}_{t,s \in T}$ as a primitive. Under the OT reformulation, $\mathbf{M}_{t \to s}$ becomes the *solution to an optimization problem* defined by $(u_t, u_s, \mathbf{N}_t, \mathbf{N}_s)$ and the cost function $c$. This is analogous to how the co-belonging operator $\mathbf{C}_t$ is defined via the resolvent — it is "derived" from $u_t$ and $\mathbf{N}_t$ via a specific computation (matrix inversion), but still enters the formal universe because it carries independent structural content.

The question is whether $\mathbf{M}$ should remain in the formal universe (with the OT characterization as its provisional realization) or be demoted to derived status (like $\mathbf{T}_t$ was in v2.0). I recommend: **keep $\mathbf{M}$ in the formal universe** but add the OT characterization as the canonical provisional realization, replacing the current §9.5.

## 9. What Is Genuinely Novel

### 9.1. Self-Referential Optimal Transport

In standard OT, the cost function $c(x,y)$ is exogenous — it depends on the geometry of the spaces $X$ and $Y$, not on the measures $\mu$ and $\nu$. Making $c$ depend on $\mu$ and $\nu$ (or equivalently, on $u_t$ and $u_s$) creates a **fixed-point problem in transport-plan space**:

$$
\pi^* = \mathrm{OT}(c(\cdot, \cdot\, ; (\pi^*)_1, (\pi^*)_2))
$$

where $(\pi^*)_1, (\pi^*)_2$ are the marginals. This has no existing theory. Key open questions:

- **Existence:** Does a fixed point exist? (Plausible by Schauder's theorem if the cost-to-plan map is continuous and compact.)
- **Uniqueness:** Is the fixed point unique? (Unlikely in general; but uniqueness may hold for small $\lambda_{\mathrm{struct}}$.)
- **Stability:** Is the fixed point stable under perturbation of the measures?

### 9.2. Partial OT with Morphological Role Constraint

Standard partial OT chooses which mass to transport based on spatial cost alone. SCC's structural cost adds a morphological constraint: transport must respect the *role* of each site (core, boundary, exterior). This is a new constraint type in the partial OT literature.

### 9.3. Wasserstein Gradient Flow with Self-Referential Energy

The JKO scheme with an energy that is itself defined through self-referential operators (Cl, D depend on $u$) is not standard. The energy $\mathcal{E}(u)$ includes terms where the "coefficients" depend on $u$ — making the gradient flow *doubly nonlinear*. This goes beyond the JKO theory, which assumes the energy is a fixed functional of the measure.

## 10. Risks and Honest Gaps

### 10.1. Self-Referential Fixed Point May Not Exist

The coupled system (fields ↔ transport ↔ cost) may not have a fixed point, or may have non-unique fixed points with no selection principle. This is the hardest open problem.

**Mitigation:** Start with the *iterative* version: fix $u$, solve OT, update $u$, re-solve OT, iterate. Even without a fixed-point theorem, the iterative scheme may converge empirically. Theory can follow computation.

### 10.2. Discrete vs. Continuous

Most deep OT results (Brenier, McCann, JKO) are stated for continuous measures on $\mathbb{R}^d$. SCC operates on finite discrete spaces $X_t$. Many results transfer (Kantorovich existence, Sinkhorn convergence, partial OT existence), but others (Brenier's theorem, regularity) need discrete analogues.

**Mitigation:** On finite discrete spaces, existence and uniqueness of entropic OT are straightforward (finite-dimensional convex optimization). The deep regularity results are not needed for the discrete theory. They become relevant only in the continuum limit.

### 10.3. Computational Cost

The entropic partial OT problem has complexity $O(n^2 \cdot S)$ where $n = |X_t|$ and $S$ is the number of Sinkhorn iterations. For the self-referential version, this is nested inside the outer field optimization. Total cost could be substantial.

**Mitigation:** Sinkhorn converges linearly; $\varepsilon$ controls the convergence rate. For small problems (the computational experiments in Iteration 3), this is tractable. For large problems, multiscale Sinkhorn or entropic regularization path methods are available.

### 10.4. The Cost Function Is Not Canonical

Three candidate cost functions (A, B, C) were proposed in §3.2. The theory does not uniquely determine which to use. This is an instance of the general "open design choice" pattern (Spec §11.2).

**Mitigation:** Option B (morphological role distance) is the minimal self-referential cost. It should be the default. Options A and C are refinements that can be investigated after the basic theory is established.
